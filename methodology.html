The Problem With AI Deployment

AI burst onto the scene in a couple of short years. Companies rushed to incorporate it under intense market pressure. Deadlines tightened. Competitors announced features weekly. Leadership demanded AI integration whether teams were ready or not.

The industry had no time to build standards, testing practices or behavioural understanding. Teams shipped models they did not fully understand. Most are still testing the wrong things - clean prompts, short examples and ideal conditions that never appear in production.

This is why 85 to 95 percent of AI deployments fail. Not because people are incompetent, but because the entire industry was pushed into a race without preparation.

If the demo was theatre, then production is reality. And reality is where the failures appear.

With sunk costs, deadlines looming and stakeholders demanding answers, development teams are now facing unpredictable instability that AI has injected directly into their systems. The question is no longer whether failures will appear - it is how fast, and how much damage they will cause.

The Solution for AI Deployment: LLM INQUISITOR METHODOLOGY

The industry is moving faster than teams can react. AI is being deployed under time, market and leadership pressure. Failures, costs and expectations are rising while successes, patience and budgets shrink.

LLM INQUISITOR METHODOLOGY gives teams the preparation layer the industry skipped:

  • real world behavioural testing that exposes failures before users do
  • stress and edge case evaluation that shows how the model behaves under pressure
  • drift detection to catch behavioural shifts before they corrupt results
  • stability and consistency checks that reveal hidden volatility
  • risk identification tied to actual workflows, not demo conditions
  • workflow design that works with the model instead of fighting it

Only by understanding the system's behaviour can companies manage the financial, legal, reputational and operational risks that AI introduces. Understanding the limitations, mitigating the risks and working with the system, not against it, is the only viable path forward.
