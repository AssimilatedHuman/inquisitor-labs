EU AI Act – System Categories

Do not be misled by the labels. Terms like high risk, limited risk and minimal risk sound narrow and technical, but in practice they cover a wide range of real systems. Many AI services operating today fall into these categories immediately, including systems that appear simple, harmless or purely assistive.

This page explains what the categories mean in operational terms so you can identify where your system sits and what obligations follow.

Important

It does not matter whether you operate a local model or rely on a service provided by a large company. The responsibility sits with you, and the obligations follow your use of the system, not the size or reputation of the provider behind it.

Why System Categories Matter

Your category determines:

  • which obligations apply
  • which documentation you must produce
  • which testing and evaluation steps are mandatory
  • whether you face high risk requirements or minimal risk freedom
  • what penalties apply if you get it wrong

Misclassification is one of the fastest ways to fall out of compliance.

Prohibited AI Systems

These systems are banned outright because they present unacceptable risks. Examples include:

  • social scoring
  • real time biometric identification in public spaces
  • systems that manipulate behaviour using subliminal techniques
  • systems that exploit vulnerable groups

If your system falls here, it cannot be placed on the EU market under any circumstances.

High Risk AI Systems

These systems are allowed but heavily regulated. High risk systems include:

  • safety critical applications
  • systems used in employment, education, finance, justice, migration or essential services
  • biometric identification and categorisation
  • AI used in medical devices or critical infrastructure

High risk systems must meet strict requirements covering data quality, documentation, testing, monitoring and human oversight. This category captures far more systems than most teams expect.

Limited Risk AI Systems

These systems must meet transparency requirements. Examples include:

  • chat interfaces
  • systems that generate or manipulate content
  • systems that interact with people in a way that could mislead

Users must be informed they are interacting with AI and given clear instructions for safe use.

Minimal Risk AI Systems

Most everyday AI falls here. Examples include:

  • spam filtering
  • recommendation engines
  • productivity assistants
  • basic analytics

Minimal risk systems have no mandatory obligations, but good practice still applies.

Foundation Models

These are large scale models trained on broad data and used for many downstream tasks. They must meet:

  • documentation requirements
  • evaluation
  • cybersecurity standards
  • reporting obligations

This applies whether you are the original developer or a downstream deployer adapting the model.

General Purpose AI

General purpose AI covers systems that can be used for many tasks across different contexts. They require:

  • documentation
  • usage instructions
  • model cards
  • transparency about capabilities and limitations

If a general purpose system is integrated into a high risk context, the high risk rules apply.

General Purpose AI with Systemic Risk

This category covers the largest and most capable models with the potential to cause widespread harm if misused. They require:

  • advanced evaluation
  • incident reporting
  • cybersecurity hardening
  • risk mitigation plans
  • continuous monitoring

This category is reserved for systems with exceptional scale and impact.

How to Identify Your Category

You can classify your system by checking:

  • what the system does
  • who uses it
  • what decisions it influences
  • whether it affects rights, safety or access to essential services
  • whether it is a standalone model or part of a larger system
  • whether it is a foundation model or general purpose AI
